1. Powerplant Electrical

1.1. Battery charging and specific gravity project

1.1.1. Overview

Project: discharge and recharge a lead acid battery Measure and document the voltage and specific gravity of each cell as the battery is charging

  • using voltmeter and hydrometer

Then, plot it on a graph showing specific gravity, voltage, and state of charge

  • make sure it's clear which cell is which

1.1.2. Hydrometer

determines specific gravity of a liquid; compares density to water specific gravity: ratio of mass of a solid or liquid to the mas of an equal volume of water works like a turkey baster small, sealed glass tube of water, weighted at the end to keep it upright, goes into the baster part inside the narrow stem is a scale from 1.1 to 1.3

  1. How to use it

    suck up just enough of the electrolyte to float the tube inside the syringe the tube will float higher in denser liquid read the scale at the surface level

    1. remove battery vent cap
    2. insert hydrometer into the first cell to be checked
    3. gently squeeze the bulb to expel the air and slowly release to draw in enough electrolyte to float the hydrometer within the syringe (but not too much, make sure the tube is not bumping on the upper edge)
    4. record the specific gravity reading for that cell
    5. replace the electrolyte
    6. replace the cap
    7. continue the process with the remaining cells
  2. Determining the state of charge

    indicated by the density of the electrolyte when the lead acid battery is fully charged, most of the sulfuric acid is in the solution in a new, fully-charged battery, the electrolyte is ~30% acid and 70% water by volume and should by 1.3 times heavier than water during discharge the sulfuric acid enters the plates, the solution becomes less dense, and its specific gravity drops below 1.3 any reading 1.275-1.300 is good any reading 1.240-1.275 is fair any reading 1.200-1.240 is bad

  3. Precautions

    temperature may affect readings and must be taken into consideration 70F-90F, no correction needed always take reading before adding water, not after this gives time for the water to mix well with the electrolyte, so you don't draw up an overly diluted sample by accident specific gravity of a cell is a reliable measure of state of charge only so long as nothing has been added to the electrolyte except small amounts of distilled water to replace that lost to evaporation be very careful with the sulfuric acid!!! it burns clothes and skin wash any exposed area thoroughly with water and apply baking soda wear splash-proof goggles and protective clothing like gloves, apron, and boots SDS are on hand

1.1.3. Charging the battery

recharge at max 3amps (make sure it's <1/2 charged to start) measure and document voltage and specific gravity every hour do not short across battery terminals battery generates hydrogen and oxygen while charging; make sure to prevent ignition! no open flames, sparks, or other sources of ignition leave caps on while charging before disconnecting or reconnecting a battery to the charger, always turn the power off first

1.1.4. Project requirements

  • should take 4-5 weeks
  • at the end of each class, flush the hydrometer out and leave it filled with water this makes it last longer
  • after the batter has been completely charged, perform a batter load test
  • complete batter discharge/charging worksheet and answer the questions
  • produce a graph of static voltage and specific gravity over the period of discharging and charging static voltage requires disconnecting from the charger the batter and letting it sit for a few minutes

1.2. Electrical power sources/storage

1.2.1. batteries

  1. overview

    container of 1 or more cells in which chemical energy is converted into electricity and used as a power source cathode and anode placed a specific distance apart and are chemically connected with an electrolyte (similar to galvanic corrosion) chemical reaction results in free electrons which can produce voltage/current batteries always produce direct current

  2. types
    1. primary cell

      not rechargable alkaline batteries eg. AA batteries no fluid inside (aka dry cell batteries) high internal resistance, irreversible chemical reaction cheap and easy to use

    2. secondary cell

      rechargable eg. lead acid, nickel cadmium, lithium ion usually identified by the material used for plates high internal resistance most common types:

      • lead-acid
      • nickel-cadmium (NiCad)

      most GA aircraft use lead-acid batteries most commercial/corporate aircraft use NiCad batteries batteries do not power the aircraft in flight; that's the alternator/generator batteries are used for:

      • ground power
      • emergency power
      • operating the starter
      • stabilizing the generator system
      1. lead acid batteries
        1. chemical reaction

          similar to automotive batteries plates become lead sulfate, electrolyte becomes water anode, cathode, and electrolyte inside the battery

          • anode is porous lead (Pb)
          • cathode is lead oxide (PbO2)
          • electrolyte is mix of sulfuric acid (H2SO4) and water

          reaction equation: Pb + PbO2 + 2 H2SO4 = 2 PbSO4 + 2 H2O

          • lead can become lead sulfate if it loses a hydrogen by sending 2 electrons through the circuit and bonds with other chemicals
          • the lead oxide can become lead sulfate if it gains the 2 electrons and hydrogen
          • the electrolyte loses the sulfuric acid and gains 2 waters
        2. cell construction

          a cell has many plates to get enough current output cells are linked in series, to get enough voltage they are connected by plate straps/cell links there are separators between plates to prevent shorts

          a typical battery has 6 cells, each cell produces ~2.1V lead acid battery cells cannot be removed

          space is left below the plates to allow for lead sulfate sediment accumulation if the sediment touches the plate, it can short out the cell

          vent plugs allow gasses to escape when upright, but prevent leakage when inverted (lead weight inside the cap) should be ~0.125" gap between electrolyte surface and bottom of the vent well

        3. battery life

          if the battery goes too low, it may not recharge generally, they will last ~5yrs specific gravity of 0.050 or more between cells means its near the end of its life battery life cycle: number of complete charge/discharge cycles a battery can perform before normal charge capacity is <80% of initial capacity varies 500-1300 cycles can be shortened:

          • over-discharging causes excess sulfation
          • too-rapid charging or discharging causes overheating –> shedding active material
          • shorting of plates, internal discharge

          eventually, even fully charged, the battery cannot supply enough power for starting

        4. battery ratings

          voltage is determined by the number of cells connecting batteries in parallel increases amp-hour capacity connecting them in series increases the voltage amp-hour rating

          • measurement of battery capacity
          • determined by multiplying current flow by time for discharge
          • function of the plate and electrolyte materials

          cold cranking amps

          • amps it can deliver at 0F for 30secs while maintaining a voltage of at least 7.72V
          • battery can only put out so much at a time (because of internal resistance)
          • this gets worse as it is discharging and as the battery gets older
          • it is a function of plate surface area

          a load tester is used to see max output performance

          • used with battery at full charge
          • full charge = 12.68V after resting (static)
          • specific gravity 1.275 or higher
          • should stay in green voltage range for 10secs
        5. servicing
          1. check state of charge

            by checking specific gravity of the electrolyte with hydrometer density of fluid compared to that of distilled water hydrometer is a glass tube with a sealed float containing a scale and water accurate between 70F-90F; otherwise you have to adjust the reading fully charged, should be about 1 part sulfuric acid to 2 parts water

            • specific gravity = 1.275-1.300
            • it will have a very low freezing point
            • cell voltage will get up to 2.113V
            • max static charge should be ~12.68V
          2. check electrolyte level

            should be 1/8" below vent well; don't overfill it should cover the plates can be adjusted by adding distilled water take specific gravity reading before adding any water! do not add electrolyte! if you spill electrolyte, neutralize with baking soda and water, rinse the area with fresh water

          3. charge the battery

            if the charging voltage is >14.2V, the electrolyte will produce oxygen and hydrogen gasses hydrogen escapes from the cell –> water loss always charge in a well-ventilated area

            • constant voltage charging voltage slightly above open-circuit voltage is placed across the terminals if it has low voltage, the charge will put a lot of current through it as it charges, its voltage goes up and the current through the battery goes down when it's fully charged, only enough current flows to compensate for loss to its internal resistance
              • voltage regulation the generator of an aircraft provides constant voltage to charge the battery the generator is regulated to have slightly higher voltage than the battery it charges the battery at a rate limited by the current limiter or the max ouput of the generator the max current flows until the battery voltage reaches that limited by the voltage regulator the charging current then drops until the battery is fully charged
            • constant current charging standard lead acid batteries should by charged slowly, with 1-2A, never exceeding 14.2V general rule: use no more than 7% of the amp-hour rating of the battery this is rarely done, which tends to kill batteries faster overcharging makes the cells flake apart, lead sulfate collects at the bottom
            • pulse/burp charging aka desulfators high voltage/current pulses break up plate sulfation it can take weeks to restore plates, but can extend their life
          4. keep it clean

            dirty battery can conduct current through grease and dirt battery and battery box should be serviced/cleaned every 100hrs/annually

          5. battery boxes

            battery boxes must be in areas approved by the manufacturer must be suitably vented battery must be secured in its box so it won't vibrate or chafe may use rubber or wooden spacers used as shims corrosion is a primary concern; any corrosion should be completely removed and touched up with acid-resistant paint remove any corrosion on cables and terminals, then apply dielectric grease fumes from the battery are corrosive, so boxes have vents to remove the fumes the battery box may have ram air routed through it to remove any fumes and carry them to a sump jar with sodium bicarb

        6. failures

          most commonly plates fail when they short or sulfate

          • they typically fail in 1 cell first
          • usually shorts internally
          • electrolyte will look black and nasty
          • cell will boil in charge and discharge

          plates or posts can break posts can corrode Dangers:

          • pressure failure they can explode
          • flash explosion they release hydrogen and oxygen; they can combust
          • getting shocked
          • shorting
          • hot conductor, fire
        7. disposal

          batteries contain hazardous material; don't put them in the garbage! most are recyclable must be taken to a Hazardous Waste disposal facility

        8. new batteries

          batteries shipped in a wet state must be maintained in a state of charge, even before use so, often they are shipped dry charged with the electrolyte separate to activate a dry charged battery:

          • remove seals from each cell
          • fill each cell to the correct level with the included electrolyte
          • ready for immediate use, but it is recommended to give it a freshening charge first
          • allow it to sit ~1hr, check the electrolyte level in each cell
          • charge at the rate specified by the manufacturer until the specific gravity remains constant in three consecutive checks at 1hr intervals
          • get rid of any extra electrolyte, don't save it dilute it a lot, and dispose of properly
          1. installation

            always disconnect the ground first and install it last write the date of installation on the battery don't short the terminals!

      2. valve-regulated lead-acid batteries (VRLA)

        similar to lead acid batteries, except all electrolyte is absorbed in glass-mat separators with no free electrolyte aka sealed batteries when charging, oxygen combines chemically with the lead to form lead sulfate and water this oxygen recombinations suppresses the generation of hydrogen overall, there is no water loss during charging, so no provisions are made for water replenishment battery cells havea pressure relief safety valve to vent if it's overcharged may have quick disconnect for easy installation and removal attaches the battery leads in the aircraft to a receptacle mounted on the side of the battery prevents accidental shorting when working with it

      3. nickel cadmium batteries
        1. usually interchangeable with lead acid type batteries

          when replacing a lead acid battery with a nicad, the battery compartment must be clean, dry, and free of all traces of acid must be washed out and neutralized with ammonia or boric acid solution, and allowed to dry completely must be painted with an alkali resistant varnish the pad in the battery sump jar should be saturated with a 3% (by weight) solution of boric acid and water before connecting it to the system

          the chemistry of lead acid and nicad batteries is incompatible one electrolyte will contaminate the other shops should have two separate servicing areas for the different types of battery maintain separate: charging systems cleaning facilities installation tools

        2. overview

          able to charge and discharge at high rates without the voltage drop associated with lead acid batteries much more expensive than lead acid operating temps -65F to 165F most commonly used on turboprop, turbojet aircraft they consist of individual, interchangeable cells and may be serviced individually can be assembled into 12V or 24V configurations, connected by cell-links 9 or 10 cells –> 12V 19 or 20 cells –> 24V cell links must be properly torqued, or else they can get overheated and show burn marks cells are housed in epoxy-coated steel cases

        3. construction

          cathode is nickel hydroxide anode is cadmium hydroxide separators are made of nylon and cellophane electrolyte is a 30% (by weight) solution of potassium hydroxide (KOH) and distilled water the electrolyte is only a conductor, does not participate in the chemical process therefore, specific gravity readings are pretty useless; will remain constant around 1.24-1.30 at 80F KOH is a strong base (alkali) it is dangerous, will damage skin, eyes, and clothing if spilled, neutralize it with boric acid and water, or with vinegar

        4. discharging

          when a load is connected across the battery electrons leave the negative plate and entre the positive plate throuh the conductor and load oxygen is driven from the positive plate and is recovered by the negative plate as it discharges, the electrolyte is absorbed by the plates and the level drops in the cell so, only top them off with water when they are completely charged!

        5. charging

          oxygen is driven from the negative plate, leaving just cadmium the positive plate accepts the oxygen and becomes more oxidized charging continues until all of the oxygen is removed from the negative plate near the end of the charge, it will gas when the water in the electrolyte is decomposed hydrogen gas is released, causing a loss of some water in the electrolyte at full charge, the electrolyte level is at its highest point when it is fully charged, it gets hot and the electrolyte bubbles and spews out the top throug the cell vent this leaves a deposit of potassium carbonate, a white powder

        6. condition of charge

          nicad batteries maintain a constant voltage almost up to the point of complete discharge the electrolyte does not participate in the chemical processes so, there is no simple way to determine the condition of charge of the battery the only way you know how much charge it has, is if you know how much you put into it

        7. thermal runaway

          nicad batteries have a very low internal resistance (IR) this is why they can charge/discharge so quickly also, voltage and resistance vary inversely with temperature so, with an increase in cell temperature, voltage and IR will decrease rise in cell temperature could be:

          • rapid discharge from high ambient temperatures in a poorly ventilated installation
          • if the cellophane breaks down in the separator material, allowing oxygen to unite with cadmium this reaction can generate enough heat to dangerously decrease the IR

          typically, aircraft have a fault protection system that monitors battery condition turbine starters need a lot of energy to start if the aircraft is having difficulty starting the battery can get too hot the center cells in particular get hot in a hung start, wait a bit for it to cool off with lowered resistance, the cells accept all the current coming from the generator, exacerbating the problem cells can break down, catch fire, explode

        8. servicing
          1. inspection in the airplane

            check batteries ~ every 50hrs batteries should be carefully monitored check that the battery box is free of contamination ventilation system clear and operating jar in the sump saturated with boric acid solution look for spilled electrolyte loose connectors, signs of arcing check/adjust electrolyte (in the shop)

          2. inspection in the shop

            wash battery:

            • check vent caps to prevent water entering the cels and flush the top of the battery with tap water
            • remove any deposits with non-metallic bristle brush
            • air dry completely

            check the leakage current

            • any conduction between the cell terminal and the case can cause it to self-discharge
            • check for current, not voltage
            • set meter to highest scale
            • place positive lead on positive terminal and negative lead on battery case
            • decrease scale until you detect current
            • any current >100mA is too much, battery should be disassembled and cleaned

            charge battery

            • use a constant current charger or special charger/analyzer as recommended by manufacturer
            • if you use a constant voltage charger, make sure to follow manufacturer instructions it is fully charged when the current tapers off and stabilizes
            • the constant current method is really preferred; slower but more effective

            adjust the electrolyte

            • wait until it's fully charged, when electrolyte is at the highest level make sure to adjust the level immediately after charging
            • adjust the electrolyte to the recommended level (~1/4" above top of plates)
            • add only distilled water, no potassium hydroxide only water is lost, so only water should be replaced

            discharge battery

            • only way to know the state of the battery is to discharge it and measure its amp-hr capacity
            • battery is connected to a load bank and discharged at its one or two hour rate until it has an average voltage of 1.0V/cell
            • record the time that this takes if <70% of the time specified by the manufacturer, the battery capacity has decreased and it needs equalization equalization: rebalancing the cells

            recharge battery

            • after rest period, charge the battery
            • monitor voltage and temperature of each cell to identify any faulty ones

            final check

            • check torque on cell links
            • check electrolyte level
            • check leakage current between cells and case
            • update the records any time it is serviced, make a record entry of the battery condition this allows trend tracking, to see problems before they get out of hand should contain:
              • condition of case and cells
              • tightness/condition of cell links
              • electrolyte level and water added to each cell
              • voltage of each cell at end of charge
              • mention any hot spots or conditions to note on later inspections

1.3.2. Structure of matter

matter: anything with mass and volume states of matter:

  • solid
  • liquid
  • gas
  • plasma

element: substance that cannot be broken down further by chemical means atom: smallest particle that will retain the properties of the element neutrons and protons in the nucleus, electrons in the cloud molecule: 2 or more atoms joined together compound: molecule that contains at least 2 different elements

forces within an atom:

  • electrostatic force polarized force between electrons and protons holds atoms together and allows them to form bonds (?)
  • magnetic force
  • gravitational force

Electrons

  • exist in shells around the nucleus
  • valence electrons form chemical bonds
  • allows current to run through conductors
  • valence electrons = 2n2 n is the shell level

1.3.3. Conductors, Semiconductors, Insulators

  • number of electrons in outer shell determines its conductivity
    • conductors (eg copper) have very few valence electrons
    • semiconductors (eg silicon) have a half full valence shell
    • insulators (eg argon) have a full valence shell
  • they often combine to achieve a complete valence shell
  1. Conductors

    they share their valence electrons atoms with few, loosely bound valence electrons give them up or transfer them easily gold, copper, silver have free electrons when they bond together with themselves, they make good conductors

  2. Semiconductors

    don't always combine with themselves sometimes will share their free electrons with other elements (covalent bonding)

    • this may affect the conductivity of the resultant element

    by mixing certain elements we can makea semiconductor that can vary its conductivity silicon, germanium most widely used semiconductors

  3. Insulators

    inert atoms with full valence shells glass, ceramic, plastic, noble gasses

1.3.4. Electromagnetic Forces

  • north/south polarity
  • only exists when electrons are moving in coordinated current
    • some materials can retain magnetic properties without current
    • different in this way from electrostatic force
  • magnetic field surrounds any permanent magnet or moving magnetic charge
  • magnetic polarity established by direction of force lines in an object
    • flows from north to south end
  • running current generates a magnetic field, and magnetic field can generate current
    • iron is really good at this
  • Electromotive Force
    • electrons can by moved by friction, magnetism, or a chemical reaction
    • electromotive force (EF) pushes them
    • this movement of electrons is called current (I) measured in amps
    • electrical charge measured in coulombs
      • 1A = 1C/sec
      • 1C = 6.24x1018
  • Voltage
    • difference in electrostatic potential between two points
    • measured in volts
    • can exist without current flowing
  • Work/energy
    • 1 joule = 1 Newton-meter (N-m)
    • 1 Volt = 1 joule/coulomb
  • Resistance
    • resistors convert electric energy to heat
    • all matter has some resistance
    • measured in ohms
    • inverse relation to wire diameter, direct relation to wire length
  • Ohm's Law and Power
    • V=IR
    • P=IV
      • power is work over time
      • electrical units are Watts
      • mechanical units are HP or joules/sec

1.3.5. Circuits

  • all circuits must have
    • electromotive source
    • one or more conductors
    • load
  • may also have a controller
  • AC/DC
  • circuit types:
    • series
    • parallel
    • combined
  • Kirchoff's Law
    • Current: current is additive in parallel branches, must add to total
    • Voltage: voltage is additive in series, must add to total
  • Measuring with a multimeter
    • voltage can be measured with circuit open or closed measure it in parallel
    • current must be measured with closed circuit measure it in series
    • resistance must be measured with open circuit measure it removed from the circuit
    • continuity is basically resistance checks to make sure the circuit is not open

1.3.6. Electromagnetic Induction and Generator Theory

  • overview
    • when a conductor is moved through a magnetic field, EMF is generated in the conductor
    • when it is in a complete circuit, it induces current
  • induction
    • different ways to accomplish induction
      • fixed field: rotating conductor in magnetic field, or
      • rotating field: rotating magnetic field near a conductive coil
      • both generate voltage and, in a circuit, current
    • armature is the conductor where current is developed
  • Left Hand Rule for Generators the polarity of voltage depends on the direction in which the conductor is moved and the position of the north and south poles of the magnetic field
    • index finger: magnetic lines of flux from north to south
    • thumb: direction of conductor movement
    • middle finger: direction of electron current flow
  • Generating current
    • a coil rotating in a field generates an alternating current/voltage
  • output strength depends on three factors:
    1. speed of coil rotation since it is geared to the engine, it's not practical to keep increasing rpm
    2. number of turns of the coil this is set at manufacture; not adjustable
    3. strength of magnetic field (number of lines of flux) this is the only factor that we can play with we can do this using an electromagnet (an iron core in a coil) when we run DC current through the coil, we generate a field
  • Left Hand Rule for Electromagnets wrap fingers around coil in direction of electron flow, thumb indicates direction of magnetic flux lines
  • generators will produce AC
    • if you want DC, you need to rectify it
    • Methods of Rectification
      • Commutation attachment at the end of the armature that switches back and forth to keep current flowing in one direction used with fixed field generator positions DC generator brushes so that commutator segments change brushes at the same time the armature current changes direction always 2 commutator bars for each coil segments are positioned to maximize output produces a ripple DC output to reduce the ripple, add more coils (and a capacitor) full wave rectification
      • diode bridge rectifier (solid state) use diodes to make sure current only flows one way used with rotating field generators full wave rectifier diode like a check valve, turned on and off by applying a voltage across it
        • forward biased: acts as conductor
        • reverse biased: acts as insulator
    • AC vs DC
      • major advantage of AC is that current and voltage can be increased/decreased by transformer
        • this means you can decrease current to save on weight with thinner wires
      • an inverter can be used to turn DC to AC

  • styles of multimeter
    • switched manually switch between ranges to get most accurate reading err on the side of too high a range, work your way down
    • auto-ranging switches between ranges automatically for best reading
  • meter leads
    • red: connected to voltage/resistance or amp port positive connection
    • black: always connected to common/ground negative connection
    • probes handles
    • tips connection point at the end of the probe
  • what to check for
    • open circuit
      • may be opened with a switch
      • fuse burnt out
      • broken wire
      • other component failure
    • continuity
    • short circuit
      • high current flow
      • things burn out
    • voltage
      • 2 types of current, AC/DC
        • when reading AC, output is converted to DC equivalent (Effective Value or Root Mean Square RMS) this is <max value multiply max value by 0.707 to find effective value
          • How to Calculate RMS Vpp = voltage peak-to-peak instantaneous voltage: voltage at single point in the cycle Vi = sine of the angle x peak voltage Vrms = square root of the mean of the squares of the instantaneous voltages you can get a close guess using the Vi from equally spaced points along the curve to do it right, just use calculus
      • differential measurement across a component
      • ground referenced measurement always touch black probe to ground quick and easy way to troubleshoot a circuit allows you to find voltage of a specific point
    • resistance
      • disconnect component first
    • current
      • first, disconnect power
      • insert ammeter in series and turn power back on
      • select highest current setting and work down
      • almost never used for troubleshooting
    • diodes
      • they have polarity anode: positive lead cathode: negative lead bar on the diode indicates the cathode check them both ways for opens and shorts
      • forward vs. reverse bias forward bias: when it allows current to flow; conductor reverse bias: when it prevents current from flowing; insulator
      • LEDs
        • anode (positive) lead is longer may also have a bump
  • how to find issues
    • usually with a voltmeter and system power on
    • also, ohmmeter, power off, component removed from circuit
    • typically, don't use the ammeter function unnecessary and time-consuming can cause undue wear on components
